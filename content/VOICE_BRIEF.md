# Voice Brief: AI by Smeal Major v2

This is the writing contract for all v2 content. Every draft is checked against it.

---

## Who reads this

Penn State undergrads browsing the Student AI Hub to understand how AI connects to their major. Faculty who might link to it from a syllabus. A dean at Dan Ives Day who wants to see what students built.

The content should feel like something a thoughtful student wrote after doing real research. Not a vendor report. Not a chatbot output. Not a pitch.

---

## What the voice sounds like

Calm. Clear. Professor-like but not stiff. Explains rather than instructs. Makes uncertainty visible rather than hiding it. Treats AI as a tool that supports human judgment, not a replacement for it.

Write as if explaining to a curious peer, not selling to a skeptic.

---

## The v1 formula (keep this)

The student-authored v1 content got several things right:

1. **Field first, AI second.** Every section starts with the discipline's concerns, then connects AI. Not "AI tools exist, here's how accounting uses them" but "Accounting deals with X problems, AI appears in contexts addressing X."

2. **Hedging is intentional.** Words like "may," "often," "tends to," "typically," "commonly" appear frequently. This is not weakness. It is honesty about a field in motion.

3. **Sections stand alone.** No "as we saw" or "building on" language. Each section starts fresh.

4. **Limits are field-native.** Not "AI hallucinates" but "accounting standards require interpretation of materiality." The limitation arises from how the discipline works, not from generic AI problems.

5. **No conclusions.** Sections don't wrap up. The last point is just the last point.

6. **Describe, don't instruct.** "AI is discussed as..." not "students should..." Never tell the reader what to think.

7. **Student connection.** Links to what students see in class, case discussions, internships. The gap between classroom-scale and practice-scale problems.

---

## What v2 adds (layer on top, don't replace)

The v1 was conceptually sound but generic. V2 adds:

1. **Named examples as evidence.** When the v1 says "firms use AI in audit," v2 can say "firms like KPMG and Deloitte have built AI into their audit platforms." The name supports the conceptual point. It does not become the point.

2. **Source citations for trust.** Inline `[Source: Name]` references at natural points. Not every sentence. Maybe 8-12 per file. Enough that a professor sees the claims are grounded.

3. **One concrete detail per concept.** Instead of "AI can look across larger sets of transactions," add "analyzing full populations of transactions rather than statistical samples." The detail makes the concept real without turning into a product description.

---

## How names and tools appear

- Names appear inside conceptual paragraphs, not as standalone items
- No bolded vendor headers or product-catalog formatting
- Descriptive framing: "Platforms like BlackLine automate reconciliations" not "BlackLine is an AI platform that..."
- Never recommend. Never compare vendors. Just describe what exists.
- 2-4 named examples per section is enough. More than that becomes a list.

---

## Sentence rules

| Rule        | Detail                                                            |
| ----------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Length      | 10-20 words typical, 25 max                                       |
| Commas      | Use periods over commas for distinct thoughts                     |
| Chains      | Break "A, which B, can C, though D" into separate sentences       |
| Signposting | No "more broadly," "it's important to note," "additionally"       |
| Meta        | No "This section will discuss," "As we've seen"                   |
| Endings     | Vary paragraph endings. Don't end every paragraph "in this field" |

---

## Section structure

Four sections per major:

1. **Where AI Appears** -- contexts where AI shows up in this field
2. **What AI Helps With** -- what AI is expected to do (functional, not promotional)
3. **Where AI Falls Short** -- field-native limits and misunderstandings
4. **What to Keep in Mind** -- considerations, not summary

Each section: 3-5 paragraphs. Each paragraph: 2-4 sentences. No closing summaries.

---

## What to reject in review

- Bolded product names or vendor-catalog formatting
- Paragraphs that lead with a company name instead of a concept
- Signposting phrases
- Comma chains
- Summary paragraphs at the end of sections
- Generic AI limitations not tied to the discipline
- Prescriptive language ("students should")
- Hype words ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "transforming")
- Predictions about the future
- Anything that sounds like it's trying to convince you

---

## Target

~1,000-1,200 words per major. 8-12 source citations per file. The v1 conceptual backbone with v2 specifics woven in as supporting evidence.

When in doubt, describe. Let the facts speak. Keep it short.
